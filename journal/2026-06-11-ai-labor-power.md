# 2026-06-11: AI, Labor, Power

### Raw Thought
What happens when AI does more of the work? Everyone says "jobs disappear," but that's too small. The real thing is bigger. If labor stops being the main thing people sell, then what happens to money, power, ownership, and what counts as a real contribution? And if new systems like web3 and agentic AI start building their own rules—their own verification, their own reputation, their own arbitration—then they're not just business models. They're new quasi-legal worlds. That's where it gets interesting and dangerous.

### What Changed
- Started: "AI will replace jobs"
- Moved to: "Labor structure weakens"
- Moved to: "Ownership and verification become central"
- Landed on: "When labor stops being the basis of power, the social contract breaks. New systems emerge to answer: how do we allocate value now?"

Each step made the thing sharper. Not just a problem. A structural rupture.

### What Holds
The core insight: people aren't afraid of AI because they're stupid. They're afraid because they correctly sense that the entire basis of how humans participate in the economy is shifting. 

And yes—new systems (tokens, milestone verification, reputation graphs, arbitration instead of courts) aren't just clever tech. They're attempts to build new frameworks for ownership, legitimacy, and power before the old ones collapse.

That's real. Not hype.

### What's Weak
I kept blurring three different kinds of scarcity:
- **real scarcity**: stuff that's actually limited (water, energy, land, compute)
- **political scarcity**: stuff limited by power, bad allocation, or capture
- **geographic scarcity**: scarcity tied to place

I was often right in instinct but wrong in precision. The fixes are different for each.

Also: I treated "taste" and "verification" like they automatically create legitimacy. They don't. They just move the capture point. New systems can still be captured. They'll just have better branding.

And I sometimes overbundled: law + AI + power + money + sovereignty + tokens into one sentence. The connection is real, but the argument gets lost if I don't separate the layers.

### What's Next
Real questions:
- How do you prevent new systems from just reproducing old inequality under cleaner language?
- What actually replaces wage labor as the basis for human power and claim?
- If AI increases total output but ownership stays concentrated, is that growth or just faster extraction?
- What does a real social contract look like when machines do most of the work?

These don't have clean answers. That's why they matter.
