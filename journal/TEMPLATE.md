## [DATE]

### Raw Thought
What you were actually trying to say. The messy version.

### What Changed
Where your view shifted. What you rejected. What became clearer.

### What Holds
The claim that survived pressure. Why it's strong.

### What's Weak
Where it breaks. What you can't prove yet. What needs separation.

### What's Next
The question that remains open. Where to dig deeper.

---

## Example Entry

**2026-06-11**

### Raw Thought
If AI does more of the work, the real issue isn't jobs. It's who controls money, ownership, verification, and what counts as a contribution when labor stops being the main bargaining chip.

### What Changed
Started with "AI takes jobs" (too small) → moved to "labor structure weakens" → moved to "ownership and verification become the bottleneck" → landed on "the social contract breaks when labor stops counting."

Each move made the claim sharper and more structural.

### What Holds
The insight that new systems (web3, agentic AI, tokenized coordination) aren't just business models—they're attempts to answer: "how do we allocate value and power when the old wage-labor structure stops working?"

That's a real structural question, not hype.

### What's Weak
I blurred real scarcity, political scarcity, and geographic scarcity together. Those need separation because the fix is different for each.

Also: I treated "taste" and "verification" as if they automatically solve legitimacy. They don't. They just shift where capture can happen.

### What's Next
How do you prevent new systems from just reproducing old inequality under a cleaner wrapper?

What actually replaces wage labor as the basis for human power and claim?
