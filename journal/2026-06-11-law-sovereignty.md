# Law Lags Power

**Date:** 2026-06-11  
**Status:** Sharp. Getting sharper.

---

## Raw Thought

Law is rigged for the people upstairs. What happens when web3 and agentic AI become the forefront? Can they become sovereign? These rules are manufactured. Look at who doesn't get arrested—diplomats, billionaires, Epstein. And there's that Silicon Valley city being built. Countries have personalities. There's the Supreme Court, complex hierarchies. But when new coordination systems emerge, the first people to use them are often the people already best at exploiting the gaps. That's the danger.

---

## What Changed

Started: "Law is fake"  
→ Moved to: "Law is a control system"  
→ Moved to: "Control systems lag behind power"  
→ Landed on: "Who gets to write the rules before the state catches up?"

The shift: not "systems are lawless," but "who gets to shape legality while institutions are still catching up?"

---

## What Holds

**Law lags power. Always.**

When new systems emerge, law doesn't arrive first. It arrives after:
- capture
- arbitrage
- loopholes
- bespoke rules
- private arbitration
- jurisdiction shopping

If agentic systems and web3-style coordination take off, the question isn't "is it legal in the abstract?"

It's: **who writes the rules first, and who gets excluded while the rules are being written?**

That's a real question, not rhetorical.

---

## What's Weak

I sometimes jumped from "law is captured" to "so build outside law" too fast.

That's tempting but too clean.

Yes, law is often captured. Yes, private systems can route around institutions. But if you remove legal structure entirely, you don't get freedom by default. You get stronger actors, opaque rules, less recourse.

The answer is not "no law."  
It's **new law**, or at least new enforceable norms with real accountability.

---

## The Sharp Version

**When new coordination systems emerge, the first people to use them at scale are often the people already best at exploiting institutional gaps.**

That's the danger.

So the real question is not: "can a project become sovereign?"

It's:
- who gets protected by the new rules
- who gets excluded
- who gets to enforce disputes
- who gets to call it innovation instead of capture
